Research, design, software, and technical infrastructure

Working at the boundary of perception, embodiment, and systems.

Most of my work lives in private repositories, active research, or unfinished collaborations, so this site is not a full public portfolio. It is a compact orientation to the kinds of questions I keep returning to, the way I work, and what it is like to build with me.

I work where perception, phenomenology, embodiment, and designed intervention meet. Some of that work takes the form of long-form writing: clarifying concepts, tracing lineages, tightening claims, and figuring out what a question is really asking. Some of it takes the form of software and technical systems: XR environments, physiological sensing stacks, browser simulations, survey pipelines, diagnostics tooling, and research infrastructure. The through-line is the same in both cases: making difficult, ambiguous, often highly subjective phenomena more thinkable, more buildable, and more experimentally or aesthetically tractable.

Themes

Engineered perception

I am drawn to questions about altered visual phenomenology, controllable stimuli, and immersive systems that can induce or approximate unusual experience without pretending to reproduce an entire state wholesale.

Imagery, suggestion, and phenomenological variation

I keep returning to how people differ in what they can vividly experience, how expectation and framing shape reports, and how subjective variation can be handled rigorously without being flattened into noise.

Embodiment and translation

I am interested in how altered perception, soft or unstable body boundaries, and avatar logics can become interaction design, XR systems, and sometimes even therapeutic or artistic-research questions.

Morphogenesis and generative systems

Some of my work moves toward relation, transformation, and generative form: places where conceptual writing, simulation, dynamic systems, and computational experiments start to inform each other directly.

Research infrastructure

I care about the technical substrate that makes interdisciplinary work actually runnable: sensor integration, diagnostics, reproducible exports, synthetic test harnesses, documentation, and the operational discipline that keeps fragile ideas alive long enough to become useful.

Approach

I do not really split writing from engineering. Writing is where terminology gets disciplined, claims get bounded, and intuitions either survive scrutiny or fail. Engineering is where questions become instruments, interfaces, prototypes, or reusable systems. I am usually at my best when a project needs someone who can hold the messy whole together: the question, the vocabulary, the evidence, the runtime behavior, and the practical path from one to the other.

I like problems that sit between disciplines and therefore tend to get mishandled by all of them: too airy for straightforward engineering, too technical for pure theory, too experiential for narrow measurement, too conceptually loaded for product language. My instinct is usually to slow the problem down, separate what is known from what is merely suggestive, and then build the smallest useful structure that lets the question move again.

Working together

Working with me usually means working with someone who is comfortable moving between conceptual framing and implementation detail without treating either as secondary. I care about source discipline, precise language, and historical context, but I am not interested in staying abstract for its own sake. I care about prototypes, tooling, and architecture, but I do not think a system is meaningful just because it runs.

If you are trying to build or think through something that falls between research, design, software, and lived experience, that is probably the right frame for approaching me.
