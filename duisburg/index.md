## Shared bodies, inspectable systems

I come to this from cognitive science and from building XR systems where
perception, agency, biosignals, and social presence have to work in real
prototypes. In Technology for Mixed Ability Interaction, that becomes a group
question: how can disabled and non-disabled collaborators co-create shared
agency through mutable XR bodies while privacy, pacing, authorship, and repair
remain negotiable?

## Profile

I am an XR developer and researcher with an M.Sc. in Cognitive Science from the
University of Osnabrueck. My work has moved from semi-corporeal avatar systems
and immersive cyberdelics into bioresponsive VR, shared group experiences, and
open XR tooling. The consistent question is how unusual forms of embodiment can
remain inhabitable, expressive, and socially legible.

That is also a personal and collaborative question for me. Close creative work
across different sensory loads, working tempos, and access needs has made
pacing, trust, communication, and repair feel like design conditions, not
afterthoughts.

The fit is concrete:

- Cognitive science into embodied XR: my Master's thesis, supervised by David R.
  Glowacki, developed mobile VR semi-corporeal avatar systems around agency,
  social presence, weakly bounded bodies, gesture, and performance constraints.
- Research systems that run: [Viscereality](https://mesmerprism.com/projects/viscereality.html)
  turns breath, cardiac rhythm, coupled oscillators, and Quest workflows into a
  working bioresponsive VR system for shared immersive environments.
- Open instruments: [Rusty XR](https://mesmerprism.com/projects/rusty-xr.html),
  [companion apps](https://mesmerprism.com/projects/viscereality-companion.html),
  and [Polar H10 tooling](https://mesmerprism.com/projects/polar-h10.html) give
  the project a base for input routing, diagnostics, workshop operation, and
  inspectable mappings across Quest, Windows, Android, OSC, LSL, and biosignals.
- Mixed-ability direction: the PhD lets this line meet participatory HCI with
  disabled collaborators: shared bodies become a way to study negotiated access,
  chosen legibility, and mutual engagement rather than only a new XR effect.

## Thesis

Access is a relation inside the group. Ability-based design argues that
interfaces should adapt to what people can and want to do; interdependence work
adds that access is often negotiated between people, tools, environments, and
care relations (Wobbrock et al. 2011; Bennett et al. 2018).

Core question: How can mixed-ability collaborators co-create a shared body that
is legible enough to coordinate, expressive enough to support mutual engagement,
and inspectable enough to trust?

## Inclusive technology and collective engagement

The advertised research direction asks for technologies that support mutual
engagement between people with and without disabilities across play, creative
expression, work, XR, robotics, IoT, and AI-supported interaction. The group
frames that work through Accessibility, HCI, Social Development, direct
collaboration with marginalized people, and co-design.

The fit is specific:

- Distributed embodiment: Barbareschi's multiple-avatar work shows distributed
  embodiment as practical agency in public work. This project asks how
  mixed-ability groups learn the agency, visibility, and repair grammar before
  that grammar moves into robotic or telepresence contexts.
- Creative access: the group's work treats creative expression as access
  research. Creative workshops give participants room to define expression,
  authorship, and social meaning before task pressure is added.
- Communication repair: CDLI gives a model for community-led communication
  equity. If speech, silence, gesture, or non-standard speech becomes relevant,
  it belongs in turn-taking and participant-defined expression, not as a
  detached ASR benchmark.
- Contestable mediation: RC Trust makes trust a human-centered question. Here,
  trust means people can understand what the system does with their input,
  choose what becomes visible, revise mappings, and refuse automatic adaptation.

## Research arc

The arc starts with the path that brought me here, then turns that path into a
concrete mixed-ability project. The Duisburg contribution is a shared-body
research program for collective engagement in mixed-ability groups.

- 0:00-0:55: Person to question. Cognitive science gave me the language of
  perception and agency; collaborative XR work made pacing, trust, and access
  practical design problems.
- 0:55-1:55: Embodiment as interface. Altered perception, body boundaries,
  biosignals, and social presence become design variables in real XR systems.
- 1:55-3:20: Systems already built. Viscereality, Quest companion tools, Rusty
  XR, Polar routing, and public research pages share one pattern: experience
  plus instrument.
- 3:20-5:00: Group bridge. The project extends the group's line of
  avatar-mediated agency, creative access, communication equity, and trustworthy
  mediation into shared XR bodies.
- 5:00-6:15: Shared swarm bodies. Distributed bodies can be shaped by breath,
  movement, switches, voice, controllers, biosignals, or rule edits without
  requiring identical control.
- 6:15-7:20: Staged method. Creative exploration first, structured play second,
  translation to tangible, robotic, communication-aware, or teleoperated
  contexts later.
- 7:20-8:20: One starting context. Homebound and energy-limited participation
  makes pacing, sensory load, asynchronous contribution, and visibility choice
  concrete.
- 8:20-9:20: Inspectable instrument. Rusty makes mappings, logs, inputs, and
  consent settings visible enough to question and revise.
- 9:20-10:00: Contribution. A prototype family, a participatory method, and
  design knowledge for mutual engagement through expressive, negotiable shared
  bodies.

## Research route

Start with creative exploration before task pressure. Luminous and weakly
bounded VR bodies can support self-other boundary softening and connectedness,
while swarm-body work shows that size, density, distribution, and correspondence
change embodiment and agency (Glowacki et al. 2022; Glowacki 2024; Ichihashi et
al. 2024).

The staged route:

1. Creative exploration: participatory workshops discover input channels,
   comfort boundaries, visibility preferences, and expressive mappings.
2. Structured play: goals, roles, timing, and constraints test coordination,
   recovery, trust, and shared authorship.
3. Translation horizon: robotic avatars, tangible swarms, and teleoperation
   become later tests of the shared-control vocabulary.

## Home-mediated participation

One possible participant context is homebound or home-mediated participation.
ME/CFS, Long COVID, severe fatigue, and prolonged social withdrawal are not the
same thing. They do, however, make ordinary co-present participation difficult
in ways that matter for interaction design: pacing, exertion risk, travel,
sensory load, identity exposure, and the need for asynchronous contribution
(CDC 2024; NICE 2021; Kato et al. 2020).

The environment would not treat loneliness or social withdrawal. It would study
how shared presence, pacing, privacy, and authorship can be designed with people
whose access to social worlds is already mediated by home and network.

## Research instrument

Rusty is not the research question. It is the instrument that can make the
mappings inspectable: what input enters the system, what it controls, who can see
that contribution, which logs are kept, and how the shared body changes.

Transparency does not solve access by itself, but it gives participants and
collaborators something concrete to question, tune, and trust.
