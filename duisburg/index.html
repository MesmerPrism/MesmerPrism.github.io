Mixed-ability XR research

Shared bodies,inspectablesystems

I come to this from cognitive science and from building XR systems where perception, agency, biosignals, and social presence have to work in real prototypes. In Technology for Mixed Ability Interaction, that becomes a group question: how can disabled and non-disabled collaborators co-create shared agency through mutable XR bodies while privacy, pacing, authorship, and repair remain negotiable?

Profile

Why this project follows from my path.

I am an XR developer and researcher with an M.Sc. in Cognitive Science from the University of Osnabrueck. My work has moved from semi-corporeal avatar systems and immersive cyberdelics into bioresponsive VR, shared group experiences, and open XR tooling. The consistent question is how unusual forms of embodiment can remain inhabitable, expressive, and socially legible.

That is also a personal and collaborative question for me. Close creative work across different sensory loads, working tempos, and access needs has made pacing, trust, communication, and repair feel like design conditions, not afterthoughts.

Cognitive science into embodied XR

My Master's thesis, supervised by David R. Glowacki, developed mobile VR semi-corporeal avatar systems around agency, social presence, weakly bounded bodies, gesture, and performance constraints.

Research systems that run

Open instruments

Rusty XR, companion apps, and Polar H10 tooling give the project a base for input routing, diagnostics, workshop operation, and inspectable mappings across Quest, Windows, Android, OSC, LSL, and biosignals.

Mixed-ability direction

The PhD lets this line meet participatory HCI with disabled collaborators: shared bodies become a way to study negotiated access, chosen legibility, and mutual engagement rather than only a new XR effect.

Thesis

Access is a relation inside the group.

Ability-based design argues that interfaces should adapt to what people can and want to do; interdependence work adds that access is often negotiated between people, tools, environments, and care relations (Wobbrock et al., 2011; Bennett et al., 2018).

How can mixed-ability collaborators co-create a shared body that is legible enough to coordinate, expressive enough to support mutual engagement, and inspectable enough to trust?

Inclusive technology and collective engagement

Mutable embodiment as mutual engagement.

The advertised research direction asks for technologies that support mutual engagement between people with and without disabilities across play, creative expression, work, XR, robotics, IoT, and AI-supported interaction (UDE, 2026). The group frames that work through Accessibility, HCI, Social Development, direct collaboration with marginalized people, and co-design (Inclusive Technology and Collective Engagement).

Distributed embodiment

Barbareschi's multiple-avatar work shows distributed embodiment as practical agency in public work. This project asks how mixed-ability groups learn the agency, visibility, and repair grammar before that grammar moves into robotic or telepresence contexts (Barbareschi et al., 2023).

Creative access

The group's work treats creative expression as access research, not as outreach decoration. Creative workshops give participants room to define expression, authorship, and social meaning before task pressure is added (Barbareschi et al., 2024; RC Trust, 2026).

Communication repair

CDLI gives a model for community-led communication equity. If speech, silence, gesture, or non-standard speech becomes relevant, it belongs in turn-taking and participant-defined expression, not as a detached ASR benchmark (CDLI, 2026).

Contestable mediation

RC Trust makes trust a human-centered question. Here, trust means people can understand what the system does with their input, choose what becomes visible, revise mappings, and refuse automatic adaptation (RC Trust).

Research arc

From embodied systems to mixed-ability mutual engagement.

The arc starts with the path that brought me here, then turns that path into a concrete mixed-ability project. The Duisburg contribution is a shared-body research program for collective engagement in mixed-ability groups.

Research route

Exploration before task pressure.

Luminous and weakly bounded VR bodies can support self-other boundary softening and connectedness, while swarm-body work shows that size, density, distribution, and correspondence change embodiment and agency (Glowacki et al., 2022; Glowacki, 2024; Ichihashi et al., 2024).

Creative work is the first research environment because participants need room to try mappings, name them, reject them, repair them, and discover what carries meaning before efficiency pressure is added. That matches a group agenda where emerging technology is evaluated through expression, communication, participation, and real social context (RC Trust, 2025).

1

Creative exploration

Participatory workshops discover input channels, comfort boundaries, visibility preferences, and expressive mappings.

2

Structured play

Goals, roles, timing, and constraints test coordination, recovery, trust, and shared authorship.

3

Translation horizon

Robotic avatars, tangible systems, CDLI-style communication repair, and teleoperation become later tests of the shared-control vocabulary.

One possible participant context

Low-demand swarm worlds for home-mediated participation.

ME/CFS, Long COVID, severe fatigue, and prolonged social withdrawal are not the same thing. They do, however, make ordinary co-present participation difficult in ways that matter for interaction design: pacing, exertion risk, travel, sensory load, identity exposure, and the need for asynchronous contribution (CDC, 2024; NICE, 2021; Kato et al., 2020).

The environment would not treat loneliness or social withdrawal. It would study how shared presence, pacing, privacy, and authorship can be designed with people whose access to social worlds is already mediated by home and network. Remote VR methods and social VR disability work provide useful precedents, but also show why recruitment, disclosure, sensory load, and participation modes need careful handling (Mottelson et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Gualano et al., 2024).

Research instrument

Open tooling makes the mediation contestable.

Rusty is not the research question. It is the instrument that can make the mappings inspectable: what input enters the system, what it controls, who can see that contribution, which logs are kept, and how the shared body changes. In RC Trust terms, the mediation should be understandable and contestable, not merely adaptive (RC Trust).

That matters in mixed-ability work because transparency does not solve access by itself, but it gives participants and collaborators something concrete to question, tune, and trust.

Boundaries

What the system can test.

Swarm control

Human-swarm interaction is a model for influencing collective tendencies, not micromanaging every unit (Kolling et al., 2016; Brown et al., 2014).

Robotics

XR is the first design laboratory. Physical robot swarms add safety, localization, latency, and deployment constraints (Kegeleirs and Birattari, 2025).

AI

AI can suggest mappings, support turn-taking, or summarize interaction histories. It should remain transparent, reversible, and subordinate to participant-defined expression (Zhou et al., 2025).

Bioelectricity

Bioelectric-inspired graph fields are optional interaction models for coupling, repair, and local-to-global patterning, not biological prediction (Pietak and Levin, 2016; Levin, 2021).

Connectedness

The study can examine co-presence, authorship, and shared agency. It should not promise treatment for loneliness, fatigue, or social withdrawal.
