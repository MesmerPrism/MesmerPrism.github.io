Public warning and source background

Flashing Light Safety

Strobotorch is a flashlight signaling tool for point-away use. Direct staring is the wrong use, especially close up, in the dark, while intoxicated, or while otherwise impaired.

It is independent from Till Holzapfel's scientific-advisor work for Brain Candy and belongs to Strobotorch's own Android-tooling documentation.

Avoid these uses

  • Staring into the flashlight.
  • Trying to induce hallucinations, visions, meditation effects, or psychedelic effects.
  • Testing whether you or someone else is photosensitive.
  • Medical treatment, therapy, relaxation, entrainment, brainwave synchronization, gamma stimulation, fractal phototherapy, or visual training.
  • Use around people who have not agreed to possible flashing-light exposure.

Stop Immediately If

Stop the torch and move away from the light if anyone experiences discomfort, eye strain, headache, migraine aura, dizziness, nausea, anxiety, confusion, visual snow-like static, afterimages, trails, disorientation, panic, or any seizure-like symptom.

Seizure And Photosensitivity Risk

Photosensitive epilepsy is uncommon, but it is real. Flashing light and certain visual patterns can trigger seizures in some people, and some people do not know they are sensitive until an event occurs. Brightness, contrast, distance, wavelength, eyes-open or eyes-closed viewing, and flash frequency all matter.

Strobotorch uses a 3 Hz direct-viewing cap for precautionary reasons. Higher-rate modes are point-away only. Public epilepsy guidance also matters for the 60 Hz feature: some people are sensitive across a broad band, and Epilepsy Action notes that sensitivity can extend from about 3 Hz to 60 Hz.

Why Direct Staring Is The Wrong Use

Stroboscopic-light research treats flicker as a perceptually active stimulus. Reviews and experiments describe bright periodic light, often around 5-50 Hz and often viewed through closed eyes or diffusion, as capable of inducing transient visual hallucination-like phenomena: colors, geometry, motion, tunnels, grids, and other simple visual content.

A phone flashlight is far from a controlled research strobe. The safe instruction stays simple: do not stare into bright flicker.

Historical Context

Hewitt et al. (2025) is the best single review for understanding why stroboscopic light should be treated as a serious perceptual stimulus rather than a novelty effect. The review connects early subjective-vision work, clinical and laboratory stroboscope research, Brion Gysin and Ian Sommerville's Dreamachine lineage, modern Dreamachine-style public installations, and current neurophenomenology.

The practical takeaway goes the other way: bright rhythmic flicker has a long record of producing color, geometry, motion, and altered-vision reports under controlled conditions, so a consumer flashlight app should avoid direct staring and keep high-rate controls behind point-away warnings.

Frequencies Are Not Purposes

Up To 3 Hz

Conservative direct-viewing flash-rate cap used in accessibility guidance.

Strobotorch: default direct-viewing cap.

Roughly 5-30 Hz

A common seizure-risk range for photosensitive people, with individual variation. Some guidance extends the sensitivity range up to about 60 Hz.

Strobotorch: avoid direct viewing; point-away only.

8-12 Hz, Especially 10 Hz

Often used in flicker and Ganzflicker studies of transient visual hallucination-like phenomena.

Strobotorch: warning context only; no feature goal.

40 Hz

A serious research frequency in gamma-sensory-stimulation work, especially dementia-related research.

Strobotorch: safety context for flashlight signaling and diagnostics.

60 Hz

A newer translational use. A small healthy-volunteer PLOS ONE study reported EEG entrainment/synchrony from repeated 60 Hz flickering white light, and a registered open-label depression study uses an LED eye-mask/goggle-style device.

Strobotorch: safety context only; depression treatment, ADHD evidence, and controlled 60 Hz optical output remain outside scope.

Fractal Timing

Some retinal and fractal-phototherapy literature treats temporal structure itself as a variable. Strobotorch reports command-trace estimates only.

Strobotorch: command-trace context only; fractal phototherapy and optical measurement remain outside scope.

Substances, HPPD, And Visual Snow

Do not use the app while under the influence of psychedelics, cannabis, dissociatives, stimulants, alcohol, or other impairing substances. Impairment can make it harder to stop, look away, interpret distress, or keep the light pointed away from other people.

People with HPPD-like symptoms, visual snow syndrome, palinopsia, photophobia, light-triggered migraine, seizure history, or strong anxiety around visual disturbance should avoid flashing modes unless guided by a qualified clinician in a proper clinical context. A flashlight-signaling app cannot provide that context.

Strobotorch's Design Response

  • The direct-viewing flash-rate path is capped at 3 Hz.
  • Point-away modes require explicit acknowledgement and may request up to 60 Hz only as command-rate signaling or diagnostic output.
  • Audio-reactive torch output is off by default and requires the point-away gate before the torch switch is enabled.
  • The app explains microphone and playback-capture requests before Android shows its own permission screens.
  • Sessions stop when the app leaves the foreground, when playback ends, or when Stop is pressed.
  • The published scope is flashlight signaling, diagnostics, and safety documentation.
