Simulated psychedelic vision

Deep Dream

Deep Dream turns on a narrow but stubborn question: what can a machine-vision hallucination actually stand in for? DeepDream begins with Google's 2015 Inceptionism work, passes through community video systems and the Hallucination Machine, and now sits inside a wider field of cyberdelic research, mixed-reality therapy claims, and visual neuroscience. The cautious answer is that DeepDream is not a replacement for psychedelic experience, but it remains one of the clearest partial instruments for studying altered visual interpretation.

Question map

What has to be separated

DeepDream is easy to overstate because the images are already rhetorically loaded. They look trippy, hallucinatory, dreamlike, and artificial in the same glance. A careful account therefore treats simulation as a graded claim instead of a yes-or-no identity claim.

At the weakest level, DeepDream resembles some reports of altered visual perception. At a stronger level, it can overlap with selected questionnaire dimensions. Stronger still, it may shift neural, cognitive, or affective measures under controlled exposure. None of those levels automatically proves that it recreates the full pharmacological, embodied, emotional, social, or autobiographical condition of a psychedelic state.

That distinction keeps the public claim precise. Machine-vision provenance, visual phenomenology, questionnaire history, cyberdelic public discourse, media rights, and current laboratory records all have to stay visible without collapsing into one verdict.

Core question

  • Which parts of psychedelic visual phenomenology can DeepDream-like systems model?
  • Which outcome claims come from visual stimulation, not the whole psychedelic state?
  • How do source footage, network layer, optical flow, and blending shape the result?
  • Which current cyberdelic projects still belong to the DeepDream lineage?

Reading stance

  • Partial hallucination instrument, not substitute trip
  • Evidence-aware history rather than image-style nostalgia
  • Evidence split by phenomenology, measures, provenance, and current records

History

A compressed timeline

The DeepDream story does not begin with clinical VR, and it does not end with the 2015 viral image wave. It sits between older cyberdelic media cultures, public psychonaut taxonomies, deep-learning interpretability, and the newer attempt to build non-pharmacological altered-state environments.

The strongest historical reading is therefore layered. DeepDream was born as a way to inspect what a trained network had learned. Public culture made it legible as machine hallucination. Developers then made the effect temporally stable enough for video and VR. Researchers used that stability to ask limited questions about altered visual phenomenology, entropy-like brain dynamics, and cognitive flexibility. Current cyberdelic work now treats it as one early method among many.

Lineage points

  • Late 1960s to 1990s: cyberdelic discourse links computing, VR, and psychedelic culture.
  • 2011: public visual-effect taxonomies make subjective visuals easier to name and compare.
  • 2015: Google publishes Inceptionism and opens the DeepDream notebook.
  • 2015: community developers stabilize DeepDream video with inheritance, blending, and optical flow.
  • 2017: the Hallucination Machine moves DeepDream into immersive VR research.
  • 2021 to 2025: EEG, flexibility, high-level cognition, and cognitive-affective studies extend the paradigm.
  • 2024 to 2026: hallucination-class modeling, visual-context work, AI/VR virtual psychedelics, and Yale records widen the field.

Synthesis

DeepDream as a partial hallucination instrument

The useful question is not whether DeepDream is psychedelic vision. It is which visual and interpretive operations it isolates well enough to study.

Simulation is graded

Asking whether DeepDream "is" psychedelic vision is too blunt. Simulation has levels. A system can resemble a report, overlap with a questionnaire factor, recruit a related interpretive process, shift a neural measure, or become useful as a controlled stimulus without reproducing the full state. DeepDream belongs in that graded space.

That graded view refuses both dismissal and hype. DeepDream can produce visual overload, recursive texture, object drift, pattern completion, and pareidolia-like interpretation. It does not reproduce the full affective, temporal, multisensory, social, autobiographical, or pharmacological condition of psychedelic experience.

The target set predates the algorithm

DeepDream did not invent the visual field it is now compared against. Psychedelic visual reports, visionary pattern traditions, and online replication communities had already named grids, tunnels, breathing surfaces, tracers, drifting, symmetry, patterned overlays, and entity-like interpretation. That vocabulary matters because it turns "looks strange" into a more specific set of visual targets.

Community resources such as the Subjective Effect Index and replication archives are not laboratory validation. Their value is different: they show a public practice of making altered vision describable, comparable, and buildable. DeepDream enters that culture as a machine-vision analogue for one subset of those reports.

What the system actually does

The basic DeepDream move is to run an image through a trained vision network, choose a layer, and adjust the image so the selected activations grow stronger. Instead of asking the network to classify an image, the process asks the image to become more like what the network is already ready to see.

Lower layers tend toward edges, strokes, and ornamental texture. Higher layers draw out more object-like features. In early ImageNet versions, that meant eyes, fur, dog faces, towers, and animal-like fragments appearing inside clouds, leaves, buildings, and skin. The result is not hallucination in a human brain, but it externalizes a recognizable perceptual gesture: over-reading ambiguous input until the world seems to answer back.

The video pipeline is not a footnote

Still-image DeepDream became a research instrument only after the moving-image problem was solved well enough. Naively processing each frame creates unstable flicker. Community and research pipelines instead used frame inheritance, optical flow, alpha blending, and layer choices so hallucinated forms could persist across motion.

That engineering history matters. The network layer may carry the biological analogy to hierarchical vision, but optical flow and blending are pragmatic continuity devices. They shape what a participant sees, yet they are not themselves a model of visual cortex. A serious account of DeepDream VR has to credit both sides: the suggestive machine-vision metaphor and the practical video machinery that made the experience stable.

What the studies support

The original Hallucination Machine study showed that DeepDream-based VR could increase selected altered-perception ratings relative to unaltered video, including intensity, patterns, imagery, vividness, strangeness, space, and arousal. It did not show temporal-production effects, and it did not prove whole-state equivalence to psilocybin.

Later work is strongest when read in the same narrow way. The 2021 EEG study reports changes in particular entropy, complexity, and functional-connectivity measures. The 2022 and 2025 cognition papers report selected shifts in flexibility, semantic-network structure, decision dynamics, and task-specific performance. The 2025 cyberdelics study reports cognitive-affective and autonomic modulation under a DeepDream-hallucinated VR counterpart. These are meaningful findings, but they are not one general result called "psychedelic cognition."

Where the match breaks

DeepDream is naturally strong at reduced veridicality, sensory-input dependence, pareidolia, recursive texture, and object-rich intrusion. It is weaker at clean geometric form constants, multisensory cross-talk, autobiographical or symbolic scenes, emotional salience, selfhood changes, social context, and time. It also carries the biases of the networks and datasets that generate it.

Current visual-neuroscience work sharpens the mismatch. Psychedelic visual experience is tied to retinal, retinofugal, cortical, contextual, questionnaire, and report-structure questions. DeepDream touches some of that territory through visual-context analogies and model hierarchy, but it does not replace direct work on psychedelic vision itself.

Current state

What the record looks like now

As of May 20, 2026, DeepDream is no longer where generative-image culture is being pushed. It is more useful as a hinge: a method that made machine pareidolia visible, entered public culture as psychedelic machine vision, and then became one part of the cyberdelic research map.

The clinical pages split into separate records. Yale Medicine's public DeepDream VRP page remains online and still says it was last updated February 5, 2026. ClinicalTrials.gov lists the linked record, NCT06581263, as completed, with actual primary completion and completion dates of March 22, 2026, and a registry update posted on March 25, 2026. That makes it a completed study record, not evidence for an actively recruiting protocol.

Psyrreal is a different branch of the story. The University of Tartu's NCT06174285 record, mirrored by CenterWatch, is recruiting patients with depression for a trial that compares a 55-minute Psyrreal experience with a non-psychedelic VR comparator called Routine Realms, with preparation and integration around each VR session. That matters because Psyrreal has moved beyond feasibility work into a live therapeutic trial, but it is not the Yale DeepDream VRP study.

Si-PHI is another separate public thread. Yale Ventures describes it as a patient- and biomarker-informed mixed-reality platform designed to produce a controlled simulated psychedelic experience for depression, while the Ranganathan lab lists the project with immersive visuals and audio. Those pages matter because they show where cyberdelic language is going, but they do not disclose enough to collapse Si-PHI into the older DeepDream VRP lineage.

2026 reading

  • The original Google repository remains the canonical code reference, but it is archived, read-only, and was last pushed in 2022.
  • The TensorFlow tutorial is the practical modern implementation entry point.
  • DeepDream VR evidence remains useful, but newer work now includes style transfer, shader systems, model-tuned hallucination classes, and mixed-reality therapeutic platforms.
  • Yale's DeepDream VRP record, Tartu's Psyrreal trial, and Yale's Si-PHI pages should be discussed as distinct public records.

Evidence audit

What is solid, partial, and still open

The strongest reading is not a single verdict. It keeps unlike evidence types apart. Technical papers explain how the effect is made. Human studies test selected outcomes. Media sources explain public framing. Current institutional and registry pages show how the language is now being used.

Solid

  • DeepDream began as feature visualization and model inspection.
  • DeepDream VR can alter selected subjective, EEG, cognitive, and affective measures.
  • Video stabilization choices are part of the stimulus, not neutral implementation detail.
  • Psyrreal has a separate recruiting registry record for depression, so it now belongs in the current comparator map.

Partial or open

  • Motyka 2025 is important for current platform direction, but the archived material is conference and OSF material.
  • Si-PHI is documented as a platform, but its exact visual stack is not fully disclosed in the available records.
  • NCT06174285 is a trial-status source, not a result paper, so it cannot support claims about Psyrreal's therapeutic effect.
  • The field still needs better measurement of stimulus statistics such as symmetry, fractal dimension, texture class, motion field, and semantic density.

Open direction

The next useful comparison

The most productive future work would compare induced-vision methods on the same map: DeepDream, stroboscopic stimulation, Ganzfeld, shaders, style transfer, oscillator tools, VR replication systems, and actual psychedelic visual-context studies. Each method has a different causal story, different parameter space, and different phenomenology.

That comparison would let DeepDream take its proper place. It is not the endpoint of computational psychedelic simulation. It is the first widely visible machine-vision method that made the analogy feel concrete enough to argue with.
