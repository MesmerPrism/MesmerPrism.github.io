Visual cortex and form constants

Bressloff V1 Form Constants

The Bressloff visual-cortex model gives a concrete way to ask how simple activity patterns in primary visual cortex could appear as tunnels, spirals, cobwebs, lattices, and honeycombs after the retino-cortical map. The point is not to claim that a browser sketch reproduces a whole hallucination. It is to make the mathematical claim inspectable: choose a cortical planform, transform it through the visual-field map, and see whether the named Kluver-like form appears (Bressloff et al., 2002).

Claim boundary

What this model can and cannot say

The useful claim is narrow. The model says that if V1 is treated as a two-dimensional cortical sheet with orientation structure, then symmetry-breaking activity patterns can become recognizable geometric visual forms after projection into retinal coordinates. This is a strong mechanistic sketch for form constants, not a full account of psychedelic imagery, visual meaning, or a specific strobe frequency (Ermentrout and Cowan, 1979; Bressloff et al., 2002).

Flicker can induce geometric visual hallucinations, and newer work studies those reports with richer phenomenological instruments. But a V1 planform model does not automatically map "12 Hz" or "40 Hz" to one exact shape. A responsible simulator should first make the cortical pattern families tunable and visible. Only then should it try to connect input frequency, neural drive, participant state, and report structure (Rule et al., 2011; Hewitt et al., 2025).

What the page shows

  • Slow browser animations of the main planform families
  • Retino-cortical remapping from cortical stripes to visual-field forms
  • Orientation-contour glyphs drawn as short local line segments
  • Starting points for the figure presets in the Rust source lab

What it does not claim

  • No calibrated flicker-frequency prediction
  • No claim to reproduce a whole altered state
  • No clinical or therapeutic claim
  • No replacement for participant reconstruction data

Animated figure presets

Planforms through the retino-cortical map

These animations are exported from the Rust V1 planform renderer, then served as static WebP loops. They drift slowly to reveal structure, not to induce flicker. If reduced motion is enabled, the browser receives static PNG poster frames instead.

Implementation note

Why there is a source lab behind the article

The public page is deliberately simple. The animations are generated from a Rust V1 planform renderer that exposes kernel parameters, an even/odd stability scan, perturbative orientation eigenfunctions, branch selection, and a retino-cortical viewer. That renderer is the place where the model can be tuned, profiled, and compared against the paper figures.

The current source lab started from the public karacsm/V1-sim notebook and the Bressloff model equations. The next fidelity step is not more decoration; it is side-by-side calibration: expose the named paper figure presets, export orientation-channel payloads, and test whether the mathematical descriptions reproduce the forms they are meant to explain.

Reading the animations

The pattern is the argument

The cortical pattern is not what the viewer sees

A stripe in cortical coordinates can become a ring, a ray, or a spiral in visual-field coordinates because the V1 map is not a rectangular copy of the image. Near the center of gaze, retinal eccentricity and polar angle are expanded into a log-polar cortical sheet. The same wave family can therefore look qualitatively different after remapping.

Contour glyphs are orientation hypotheses

The short line overlays mark local dominant orientation channels. They are not extra particles and not separate neurons. They are a compact way to show how an orientation-resolved model can turn scalar activity into contoured hallucination sketches, which is central to the later Bressloff account.

The honest use is exploratory first

A simulator is useful here when it lets the model fail in public. If the equations cannot generate the named family under any plausible parameter setting, that is informative. If a small parameter shift moves the model from cobweb to rhombic to honeycomb, that is also informative. The point is to put the mathematical description close enough to the image that the comparison becomes checkable.
