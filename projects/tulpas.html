Tulpas, thoughtforms, and inner agency

Tulpas

Tulpas is a newly separated writing project about made inner others: tulpas, thoughtforms, headmates, servitors, egregores, imposition, trained inner sociality, psychofauna, and digital-familiar analogies. It starts from a seed essay, but the public project is narrower and more careful: it asks how practice communities stabilize agency-like experiences, how those maps cross Prophantasia, Phenomenological Control, and SANE, and which claims still need evidence before they can become finished prose.

The page does not claim that tulpas are externally real, clinically validated, or reducible to one mechanism. It treats tulpamancy as a source ecology: a hybrid historical term, an online practice culture, a plurality and headmate-adjacent discourse, a bridge to imposition, and a useful test case for writing about inner agency without flattening it.

Cross-pollination

The topic that kept showing up elsewhere

The tulpa material was already present across several writing projects, but it was doing different work in each one. In Prophantasia, it appeared mostly through imposition and projected imagery: reports where a companion is imagined or perceived as if sharing ordinary space. In Phenomenological Control, it appeared as a trained-experience problem: imagery, absorption, expectancy, porosity, voice-like events, and discernment. In SANE and the older Psychotic Warlocks material, it appeared as psychofauna: inner others, recursive agency, thoughtforms, headmates, egregores, and digital familiars.

Those crossings are useful only if they stay distinct. Imposition can help Prophantasia name a projected-imagery edge case, but tulpamancy is not a prophantasia training system. Phenomenological Control can supply variables, but it should not turn tulpas into "just suggestion" or "just hallucination." SANE can use thoughtforms and headmates to test its inner-ecology language, but psychofauna remains a local framework term until separately sourced.

A dedicated project keeps the burden of proof visible. Tibetan sprul-pa, Theosophical thought-forms, chaos-magic servitors, egregores, internet tulpamancy, plural/headmate discourse, clinical plurality, imaginary companions, and AI companions can all be compared. They cannot be treated as the same thing.

Main crossings

  • Prophantasia: imposition, projected imagery, visualization, nonvisual boundary cases, and community practice provenance.
  • Phenomenological Control: absorption, expectancy, porosity, trained attention, voice-like events, and prayer comparison.
  • SANE: psychofauna, recursive agency, thoughtforms, headmates, egregores, and digital-familiar analogies.
  • Scientific Surrealism: source discipline for strange experience and unstable knowledge-art categories.

Kept apart

  • Tibetan and Himalayan terms versus modern online tulpamancy.
  • Thoughtforms, servitors, egregores, tulpas, headmates, and imaginary companions.
  • Imposition as practice vocabulary versus Prophantasia as research map.
  • Self-report and survey findings versus clinical or causal claims.
  • Local SANE vocabulary versus established scientific category.

Source state

What is solid, partial, and still open

As of May 11, 2026, the dedicated Tulpas project is new. The local archive is strongest where Prophantasia already audited tulpamancy and imposition, and where SANE/Psychotic Warlocks already developed thoughtform and psychofauna language. The seed essay is a useful starting document, but it is not yet a finished source-verified history.

The strongest public sources support a careful map: Mikles and Laycock for the modern tulpa's hybrid genealogy, Veissiere and Laursen for online tulpamancy and participatory identity culture, Tulpa.info and Tulpanomicon for community guide vocabulary, Luhrmann/Lifshitz and Hale for prayer, voice-like experience, and imaginal discernment comparisons, and Palmer-Cooper and colleagues for a narrow observational comparison involving tulpamancy and unusual sensory experience.

The open work is equally important. The exact Tibetan, Theosophical, and occult genealogy needs a primary-source pass. Mental-health claims need to stay in the observational and self-report register unless stronger evidence is brought in. Lifshitz-related slides, talks, open-science traces, and Brainhack neuroimaging artifacts show active research interest, but they should not be presented as settled results.

Safe now

  • Hybrid genealogy and concept drift.
  • Online tulpamancy as a practice and identity culture.
  • Imposition as the strongest Prophantasia bridge.
  • Phenomenological variables: imagery, absorption, expectancy, porosity, attention, and discernment.
  • SANE psychofauna as local framework vocabulary.

Needs another pass

  • Primary-source wording around David-Neel, Evans-Wentz, Besant, Leadbeater, and Tibetan terminology.
  • Early internet chronology across /x/, Tulpa.info, Reddit, and Brony-adjacent circulation.
  • Any percentage claim about wellbeing, benefit, harm, or prevalence.
  • Neuroimaging claims beyond preliminary artifact status.
  • AI companion and digital-familiar analogies beyond SANE's local frame.

Working text

The made inner other is not one thing

This is the current public synthesis from the research pass. It is written as a map, not a final essay.

A useful instability

Tulpas are interesting because the category keeps moving. In one lane, the term points backward into Tibetan and Western esoteric circulation, where "emanation," "thought-form," occult imagination, and paranormal folklore get tangled. In another, it points to online practice communities that built guides, norms, forums, glossary terms, safety warnings, and identity positions around creating or relating to an apparently autonomous inner companion. In a third lane, it touches plurality, headmates, imaginary companions, voice-like experiences, and the question of how a person learns to recognize some inner event as not simply authored by the ordinary self.

That instability is the point of the project. A clean definition would be easier, but it would hide the reason the topic crosses so many of my projects. Tulpas force a writing method that can hold history, internet practice, altered perception, social identity, and speculative agency without letting any one of them claim the whole field.

Imposition is the Prophantasia bridge

The strongest overlap with Prophantasia is imposition. In community vocabulary, imposition names practices and reports in which a tulpa is experienced as present in ordinary space, sometimes visually and sometimes through other senses. That makes it directly relevant to projected imagery: internally generated content described as world-placed, environment-overlaid, or spatially present.

The limit matters. Prophantasia is about imagery-change and projected-imagery claims. Tulpamancy is wider. A person can relate to a tulpa through dialogue, presence, writing, narration, nonvisual knowing, wonderland practice, or identity language without treating visual imposition as the center. The bridge is real, but it is a bridge across one edge of the topic.

Phenomenological Control supplies variables

Phenomenological Control helps because it breaks the experience into tractable variables: imagery vividness, sensory modality, absorption, expectation, suggestion, porosity, attentional training, social framing, involuntariness, and discernment. That is why prayer, voice-hearing, hypnosis, imaginary companions, and unusual sensory experience appear nearby. They are not all the same phenomenon, but they all test how practice and interpretation can change what inner experience feels like.

The public claim should stay there. Tulpamancy is not explained by one psychological variable. Absorption may matter for some people. Visualization may matter for some practices. Expectation and community scripts may shape reports. None of that licenses a total reduction.

SANE gives a framework, not outside proof

In SANE, the tulpa material belongs to psychofauna: the felt population of inner voices, drives, presences, characters, habits, roles, and semi-autonomous tendencies that people describe in religious, therapeutic, creative, occult, plural, and everyday vocabularies. Tulpas, thoughtforms, egregores, servitors, headmates, and digital companions all press on the same question: when does a pattern of attention, dialogue, memory, role, and response become agency-like enough that people need a practice language for it?

That is the project's most useful speculative edge, but it is also where the evidence line has to be strict. Psychofauna is local framework vocabulary. It can organize the writing and generate research questions; it cannot be cited as an established scientific category.

Current research uptake is real but uneven

Tulpamancy has moved far enough into academic and public research discourse that it no longer makes sense to treat it only as forum folklore. There are cyberethnographic, digital-religion, cognitive-neuropsychiatric, voice-hearing, prayer-comparison, and preliminary neuroimaging threads. But the level of evidence differs sharply across those sources. Some records are peer-reviewed articles. Some are public talks or conference-style pages. Some are community guides. Some are open-science artifacts or in-progress analysis.

The writing project should make that unevenness visible instead of smoothing it away. A strong tulpa essay will be less persuasive if it sounds too certain. It will be more useful if readers can see which claims are historical, ethnographic, phenomenological, observational, local-framework, or still only speculative.

Evidence routing

Working claim lanes

The local research pass routes claims into evidence lanes so future drafts can add sources without laundering weak claims into strong ones.

Historical and community

  • Solid enough: modern tulpamancy has a hybrid genealogy and a distinct online practice culture.
  • Partial: exact historical wording and early internet chronology need primary-source and archive checks.

Prophantasia and imposition

  • Solid enough: imposition is the strongest projected-imagery bridge.
  • Boundary: many tulpamancy accounts are not visual and not centered on projected imagery.

Phenomenological Control

  • Solid enough: imagery, absorption, expectation, porosity, and discernment are useful variables.
  • Boundary: none of those variables should be treated as the total explanation.

SANE and psychofauna

  • Solid enough: tulpas are a strong local test case for inner ecology and recursive agency.
  • Boundary: psychofauna is project vocabulary, not an established external category.
